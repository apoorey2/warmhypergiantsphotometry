# Photometric Variability of Warm Hypergiants in M31 & M33

**Authors:** Ashi Poorey, Prof. John Martin   

---

## **Abstract**

Warm Hypergiants (WHG) are extremely luminous A–F type supergiants, approximately 20–40 times the mass of the Sun, located on the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram near the Humphreys–Davidson limit and the yellow void, where stars are thought to undergo unstable evolutionary transitions. Previous photometric studies of WHG, including work by Dorn-Wallerstein et al. [1], identified irregular brightness variations but were not sensitive to very long-period variability.  

This project investigates whether WHG exhibit long-term periodicity in their brightness variations with amplitudes large enough to potentially drive episodes of mass loss and evolutionary change. Twelve years of photometric data for ten WHG in the nearby galaxies **M31** and **M33** are analyzed using **Lomb–Scargle time-series methods** to search for variability on timescales from weeks to several years. The targets are grouped into at least three categories:  

1. Stars showing periods of several hundred days  
2. Stars with periods longer than 1000 days  
3. Stars with no discernible periodic variation  

These results help characterize how **pulsation and episodic mass loss** influence the late evolutionary stages of massive stars.

---

## **Project Overview**

The purpose of this project is to explore long-term photometric variability in warm hypergiants and understand its potential impact on stellar evolution and mass-loss events. This research combines archival photometric data, time-series analysis, and astrophysical interpretation to study these rare massive stars.

---

## **Data & Methods**

- **Data Sources:** Photometric observations spanning 12 years for 10 WHG in M31 and M33.  
- **Analysis Tools:**  
  - Python libraries for time-series analysis (e.g., `astropy.timeseries`, `numpy`, `scipy`)  
  - Lomb–Scargle periodograms to identify periodicity  
  - Data visualization using `matplotlib` and `seaborn`  
- **Methodology:**  
  1. Preprocess and clean photometric datasets  
  2. Apply Lomb–Scargle analysis to detect long- and short-period variations  
  3. Categorize WHG based on periodicity results  
  4. Interpret astrophysical implications regarding pulsation and mass-loss episodes  

---

## **Results**

- Identification of WHG with **periodic variability** on timescales from several hundred to over 1000 days  
- Stars with no significant periodicity were also documented  
- Analysis provides insights into **how pulsations may drive mass loss** and influence late-stage stellar evolution
